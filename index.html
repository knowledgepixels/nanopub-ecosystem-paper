Background and Related Work

The nanopublication was introduced by Groth et al. [4] as a minimal unit of publishable knowledge that couples a single assertion with machine-readable provenance and publication-information metadata. Subsequent work broadened the scope of the concept beyond its biomedical origins [5] and articulated a broader vision of genuine semantic publishing in which scholarly artefacts are first-class, machine-actionable entities on the web [7]. A community survey documented the subsequent growth of nanopublications as a provenance-centric resource of scientific linked data [10].

Identity, verifiability, and immutability of such artefacts are addressed by Trusty URIs [14], which embed a content-derived cryptographic hash into the URI itself so that every retrieval is self-verifying; the mechanism was generalised and analysed for arbitrary digital artefacts in [8]. These building blocks underpin the trust model of the ecosystem presented here.

The paradigm of publishing without central gatekeepers was first outlined in [11] and subsequently realised as a running decentralized network of nanopublication servers with end-to-end provenance [6]. More recent work added a layer of decentralized services that enable semantic micro-contributions on top of that network [13].

For evolving resources, reliable granular references to changing linked data [12] allow precise citation of parts of mutable datasets, and dedicated frameworks and formats for citing nanopublications themselves have been proposed [2,3].

At the tooling and application level, the nanopub-java library provides a reference implementation for producing, validating, and processing nanopublications [9]. Representative data-centric applications include the DisGeNET gene–disease resource [15] and a provenance-centered drug–drug interaction dataset [1].
